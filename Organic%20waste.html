Organic waste

Biodegradable waste includes any organic matter in waste which can be broken down into carbon dioxide, water, methane or simple organic molecules by micro-organisms and other living things by composting, aerobic digestion, anaerobic digestion or similar processes. In waste management, it also includes some inorganic materials which can be decomposed by bacteria. Such materials include gypsum and its products such as plasterboard and other simple organic sulfates which can decompose to yield hydrogen sulphide in anaerobic land-fill conditions.

Climate change impacts

Landfill gas is a mix of different gases created by the action of microorganisms within a landfill as they decompose organic waste, including for example, food waste and paper waste. Landfill gas is approximately forty to sixty percent methane, with the remainder being mostly carbon dioxide. Trace amounts of other volatile organic compounds (VOCs) comprise the remainder. These trace gases include a large array of species, mainly simple hydrocarbons.

Food loss and waste is food that is not eaten. The causes of food waste or loss are numerous and occur throughout the production, processing, distribution, retail and consumption. Overall, about one third of the world's food is thrown away.

Steps to reduce organic waste

  • Learn to compost at home.
  • Use insulation made from recycled paper, glass, and other recovered materials.
  • Think green before you shop.
